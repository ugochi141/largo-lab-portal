1. What is SBAR?

SBAR (Situation-Background-Assessment-Recommendation) is a standardized communication framework used throughout healthcare to improve clarity and patient safety. Originally developed by the U.S. Navy for nuclear submarines, it was adapted for healthcare in the 1990s and is now considered a best practice by The Joint Commission.

üéØ Purpose of SBAR

SBAR provides a predictable structure for communication, ensuring that critical information is conveyed efficiently and completely. This is especially important in high-stress situations where clear communication can impact patient outcomes.

At Kaiser Permanente Largo Laboratory, SBAR is used for:

  • Shift handoffs between laboratory staff
  • Communication with nurses and physicians about critical values
  • Reporting equipment malfunctions or safety issues
  • Escalating concerns to management
  • UBT (Unit-Based Team) action requests
  • Supply chain and inventory urgent requests

3. SBAR Components Explained

S - Situation

What is happening RIGHT NOW?

  • Identify yourself and your role
  • State the patient/equipment/issue
  • Describe what's happening in 1-2 sentences
  • Be concise and specific

Example:
"This is Tracy from Largo Lab Processing. Our Sysmex XN-2000 in the AUC lab is showing a recurring Aperture Error 3201 and cannot complete CBC analysis."

B - Background

What led to this situation?

  • Provide relevant history and context
  • Include dates, times, previous attempts
  • Share pertinent patient data or equipment history
  • Keep it factual and chronological

Example:
"This error started at 7:45 AM during morning QC. We've attempted 3 Startup/Shutdown cycles and cleaned the aperture bath twice per SOP. The instrument passed all QC yesterday on day shift."

A - Assessment

What do YOU think is going on?

  • Share your clinical judgment or professional opinion
  • Identify severity and urgency
  • State the impact on operations
  • Use critical thinking

Example:
"I believe this is a hardware failure requiring field service. We have 47 pending CBC orders in Epic Beaker. If not resolved within 1 hour, we'll need to divert specimens to Landover."

R - Recommendation

What should we DO about it?

  • Propose specific actions
  • Suggest who should do what and when
  • Offer alternatives if applicable
  • Be clear and direct

Example:
"I recommend: (1) Call Sysmex field service immediately (800-232-1205), (2) Activate backup processing protocol using Landover for stat CBCs, (3) Notify nursing via Qmatic of 30-minute delay for routine CBCs."

4. When to Use SBAR

Always Use SBAR For:

  • Critical Value Reporting: When calling critical/panic values to providers
  • Equipment Failures: Major analyzer malfunctions affecting patient care
  • Shift Handoffs: Transitioning complex situations to the next shift
  • Safety Concerns: Reporting potential patient safety issues
  • Escalations to Management: When issues require supervisor/manager intervention
  • UBT Action Requests: Proposing improvements or changes
  • Urgent Supply Needs: Critical reagent shortages affecting testing
  • Staffing Crises: When staffing levels create patient care risks

Consider Using SBAR For:

  • Complex patient cases requiring discussion
  • Procedure clarifications with supervisors
  • Interdepartmental communication about workflow issues
  • Training scenarios to practice structured thinking
  • Email communication requiring clear action items

When NOT to Use SBAR:

‚ö†Ô∏è SBAR is Not Required For:

  • Routine daily check-ins or casual updates
  • Simple yes/no questions
  • General information sharing without action needed
  • Social conversations or team-building discussions

Tip: If a situation requires immediate action or involves patient safety, use SBAR. When in doubt, using SBAR is always better than missing critical information.

7. Real-World Laboratory Examples

Example 1: Equipment Malfunction

S - SITUATION: "This is Tracy calling from Largo Lab Processing. Our Sysmex XN-2000 hematology analyzer in the AUC location is showing recurring Aperture Error 3201 and cannot complete CBC analysis."
B - BACKGROUND: "The error first appeared at 7:45 AM this morning during routine morning QC. We've attempted 3 complete Startup/Shutdown cycles per troubleshooting protocol and cleaned the aperture bath twice according to SOP. The instrument passed all Level 1, 2, and 3 QC yesterday on day shift without any issues. We have not had any power outages or environmental changes overnight."
A - ASSESSMENT: "Based on the persistent error after proper troubleshooting and the fact that QC passed yesterday, I believe this is a hardware failure requiring Sysmex field service intervention. We currently have 47 pending CBC orders in Epic Beaker with 12 marked as STAT. If we cannot resolve this within 1 hour, we risk significant delays in patient care and may violate our TAT targets."
R - RECOMMENDATION: "I recommend the following immediate actions: (1) Call Sysmex field service at 800-232-1205 and request emergency service, (2) Activate our backup processing protocol by routing all STAT CBCs to Landover Lab, (3) Notify ED and ICU nursing via Qmatic messaging of a 30-minute delay for routine CBCs, (4) Document this incident in the equipment maintenance log for compliance purposes. Can you authorize the field service call and approve the backup protocol?"

Example 2: Staffing Shortage Crisis

S - SITUATION: "This is Netta, day shift lead at Largo Lab. We have 3 unexpected call-outs this morning leaving us with only 2 Medical Laboratory Scientists and 1 MLT for a Monday morning shift."
B - BACKGROUND: "Tracy called out at 5:45 AM (sick), Boyet called out at 6:15 AM (childcare emergency), and Jackie just called at 6:30 AM (car accident - non-injury but car not drivable). Our normal Monday staffing is 5 MLS, 2 MLT. We have 3 floors of inpatients, a full ED, and morning phlebotomy rounds just starting. We typically process 400-500 specimens on Monday mornings between 6 AM and 11 AM."
A - ASSESSMENT: "With only 3 lab staff, we cannot safely cover processing, QC/maintenance, and technical support for 3 hospital floors. STAT specimens from ED and ICU will be prioritized, but routine morning labs will be significantly delayed. This creates patient safety risks as morning physician rounds depend on overnight lab results for clinical decision-making. We're also at risk of violating CLIA staffing requirements for supervision ratios."
R - RECOMMENDATION: "I recommend: (1) Call in Booker on overtime to cover processing (he's agreed to come if called), (2) Request a floater MLS from Landover or Capitol Hill labs if available, (3) Notify hospital administration and nursing supervisors of potential delays, (4) Postpone all non-critical maintenance until tomorrow, (5) I will personally extend my shift by 2 hours to cover the gap. Please approve overtime for Booker and coordinate with other labs for potential float staff."

Example 3: Critical Supply Chain Issue

S - SITUATION: "This is Emma calling from Largo Lab Chemistry section. We are down to our last 2 bottles of Roche Cobas Electrolyte Reagent Pack (Ref 04460715190) and will run out by tomorrow morning at 10 AM based on current specimen volume."
B - BACKGROUND: "Our normal PAR level is 10 bottles with reorder point at 4 bottles. We placed a routine order with Largo Inventory Team (LargoInventoryTeam@KP.org) on October 28th, but received notification yesterday that Roche has a national backorder on this reagent due to manufacturing issues at their Indianapolis facility. The expected ship date was November 5th but has been pushed to November 12th. We use approximately 1 bottle per day with Monday-Wednesday being our highest volume days (1.5 bottles/day)."
A - ASSESSMENT: "Electrolyte testing (Sodium, Potassium, Chloride, CO2) is critical for patient care across all departments‚Äîespecially ED, ICU, and dialysis patients. Without reagent, we cannot perform these tests in-house. Sending specimens to Landover would add 2-3 hours to turnaround time, which is unacceptable for critical care patients. This could impact patient safety and delay clinical decision-making for approximately 200 patients per day."
R - RECOMMENDATION: "I recommend urgent action on three fronts: (1) Contact Alex Roberson or Erick Albarracin at Largo Inventory immediately to request emergency procurement from an alternate distributor or regional KP lab with surplus stock, (2) Notify Medical Director Dr. Chen and Laboratory Director to alert clinical staff of potential service disruption, (3) Prepare backup plan to courier all electrolyte specimens to Landover starting November 4th if reagent not secured, (4) Investigate possibility of borrowing reagent from Capitol Hill or Landover labs if they have surplus. Can you authorize emergency procurement and coordinate with other labs?"

8. Best Practices

‚úÖ DO: Communication Best Practices

  • Be Prepared: Gather all relevant information before starting your SBAR
  • Use Numbers: Quantify whenever possible (times, specimen counts, error rates)
  • Be Specific: Use equipment names, model numbers, specific times, and exact locations
  • Stay Factual: Present objective information without emotional language
  • Think Critically: In Assessment, use your professional judgment and expertise
  • Propose Solutions: Always offer at least one concrete recommendation
  • Follow Up: Document the SBAR communication and any actions taken
  • Practice Regularly: Use SBAR even for less urgent situations to build the habit
  • Request Acknowledgment: End with a question to confirm understanding ("Can you approve this?" or "Do you agree with this plan?")
  • Time It Right: For phone calls, ask "Is now a good time?" before launching into your SBAR

üìû SBAR Phone Call Protocol

For phone communications using SBAR:

  1. Identify yourself: Name, role, department, location
  2. Verify recipient: "Is this Dr. Smith?" or "Am I speaking with the charge nurse?"
  3. State urgency: "I need to report a critical value" or "I have an urgent equipment issue"
  4. Check readiness: "Do you have a moment?" or "Are you able to take this information now?"
  5. Deliver SBAR: Go through S-B-A-R systematically
  6. Get confirmation: "Can you read that back to me?" for critical values
  7. Document: Record time, recipient, and outcome

9. Common Mistakes to Avoid

‚ùå DON'T: Common Pitfalls

  • Don't Ramble: Keep each section concise. SBAR should take 2-3 minutes to deliver verbally
  • Don't Skip Sections: All four components (S-B-A-R) are essential‚Äînever omit any
  • Don't Be Vague: "The machine is broken" ‚Üí Instead: "Sysmex XN-2000 showing Aperture Error 3201"
  • Don't Omit Assessment: Don't just report facts‚Äîshare your professional opinion
  • Don't Forget Recommendations: Never end SBAR without proposing at least one action
  • Don't Use Jargon: When communicating outside the lab, explain technical terms
  • Don't Assume Context: Provide enough background even if you think they "should know"
  • Don't Get Emotional: Stay professional and factual, even in stressful situations
  • Don't Wait Too Long: SBAR urgent issues immediately‚Äîdon't delay
  • Don't Fail to Document: Always document SBAR communications in appropriate logs

Before and After Examples

‚ùå POOR SBAR

"Hey, the machine isn't working and we have a bunch of samples backed up. Can you call someone to fix it? This is really bad and I don't know what to do. We tried turning it off and on. Someone should really do something about this soon."

Problems:

  • No identification of self or equipment
  • Vague and emotional language
  • No specific timeline or impact
  • No clear recommendation
  • Unprofessional tone

‚úÖ GOOD SBAR

S: "This is Tracy from Processing. Sysmex XN-2000 in AUC showing Error 3201."

B: "Error started 7:45 AM during QC. Attempted 3 restarts and aperture cleaning. Passed QC yesterday."

R: "Call Sysmex at 800-232-1205, route STATs to Landover, notify nursing of 30-min delay."

Strengths:

  • Clear identification
  • Specific equipment and error code
  • Quantified impact (47 samples, 12 STATs)
  • Concrete recommendations with phone number
  • Professional and concise

10. Training Recommendations

New Employee Orientation

Week 1 - Introduction:

  • Provide SBAR Flyer and Pocket Reference Card during orientation
  • Explain the purpose and importance of structured communication
  • Review the portal's SBAR templates and when to use them
  • Have new hire observe experienced staff using SBAR during shift

Week 2-4 - Practice:

  • Role-play SBAR scenarios during huddles (use portal examples)
  • Have new hire write SBAR for hypothetical situations
  • Pair with mentor to practice SBAR during actual situations
  • Provide feedback on SBAR structure and content

Week 5+ - Independence:

  • New hire uses SBAR independently with supervisor spot-checking
  • Include SBAR competency in 90-day evaluation
  • Continue to reinforce SBAR in daily operations

Ongoing Staff Development

Monthly Reinforcement Activities:

  • Case Studies: Review real SBAR communications from the past month (with PHI removed)
  • Skills Drills: 5-minute SBAR practice scenarios during staff meetings
  • Recognition: Acknowledge staff who demonstrate excellent SBAR communication
  • Refreshers: Quarterly review of SBAR components and best practices
  • Feedback Loop: Encourage staff to share challenging SBAR situations for group discussion

UBT Integration

Incorporate SBAR into Unit-Based Team (UBT) activities:

  • UBT Action Requests: Require SBAR format for all improvement proposals
  • Huddle Practice: Use first 5 minutes of weekly huddle for SBAR skill drills
  • Safety Reporting: Use SBAR for all patient safety event reports
  • Performance Metrics: Track SBAR adoption as a team performance indicator

Competency Assessment

‚ö†Ô∏è Annual SBAR Competency

All laboratory staff should demonstrate SBAR competency annually:

  1. Written Assessment: Complete SBAR form for a provided scenario
  2. Verbal Assessment: Deliver SBAR verbally during mock phone call
  3. Observed Competency: Supervisor observes SBAR use in real situation
  4. Peer Review: Staff review each other's SBAR documentation

Passing Criteria: Must correctly include all 4 components (S-B-A-R) with specific, actionable information
