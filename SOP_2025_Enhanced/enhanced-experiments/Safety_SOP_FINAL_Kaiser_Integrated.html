1. PURPOSE

This comprehensive safety protocol establishes standards and procedures to ensure a safe working environment for all laboratory personnel, protect patients and visitors, and maintain compliance with federal, state, and Kaiser Permanente safety regulations. This document serves as the primary reference for all laboratory safety practices.

Safety Program Objectives

  • Eliminate workplace injuries and exposures
  • Ensure compliance with OSHA, CAP, and Joint Commission standards
  • Foster a culture of safety awareness and responsibility
  • Provide comprehensive safety training and resources
  • Maintain emergency preparedness at all times
  • Protect staff, patients, and the environment from laboratory hazards

2. SCOPE

This safety protocol applies to:

  • All laboratory personnel regardless of shift or position
  • Students, observers, and temporary staff
  • Maintenance, housekeeping, and support staff entering the laboratory
  • Vendors and service technicians
  • All laboratory areas including main lab, POCT sites, and MOB laboratory

4. BIOLOGICAL SAFETY

Universal Precautions - Treat ALL Specimens as Potentially Infectious

  • Assume all blood, body fluids, and tissues contain bloodborne pathogens
  • Use appropriate PPE for all specimen handling
  • Never recap, bend, or break contaminated needles
  • Dispose of sharps immediately in designated containers
  • Decontaminate work surfaces after procedures and spills

Biosafety Level 2 (BSL-2) Practices

Practice Requirement Implementation
Access Control Restricted to trained personnel Badge access, visitor log
Biological Safety Cabinet Use for aerosol-generating procedures Class II BSC in microbiology
Decontamination 10% bleach or approved disinfectant Daily and after spills
Sharps Safety Safety-engineered devices Retractable needles, safety lancets
Specimen Transport Leak-proof containers Secondary containment required
Waste Disposal Red bag/sharps containers Contracted medical waste service

High-Risk Specimens Requiring Extra Precautions

  • Suspected prion disease (CJD) - Special decontamination required
  • TB specimens - Process in BSC, N95 respirator required
  • Select agents - Notify supervisor immediately
  • Unknown rashes or lesions - Contact Infection Control

5. CHEMICAL SAFETY

Chemical Hygiene Plan Requirements

  • Maintain current Safety Data Sheets (SDS) for all chemicals
  • Label all containers with contents and hazards
  • Store chemicals according to compatibility
  • Use fume hood for volatile substances
  • Never pipette by mouth
  • Conduct annual chemical inventory
Chemical Class Examples Storage Special Precautions
Corrosives Acids, bases Separate cabinets Acid-resistant PPE
Flammables Alcohols, acetone Flammable cabinet No ignition sources
Oxidizers Bleach, peroxide Away from organics Prevent contamination
Toxics Formaldehyde, xylene Ventilated storage Fume hood use
Carcinogens Certain stains Designated area Special training required
Compressed Gases CO2, N2 Secured upright Regulator safety

Chemical Spill Response

  1. ASSESS: Determine spill size and hazard level
  2. ALERT: Notify others in area, evacuate if necessary
  3. CONTAIN: Use spill kit appropriate for chemical type
  4. CLEAN: Follow SDS cleanup procedures
  5. DISPOSE: Place waste in appropriate container
  6. DOCUMENT: Complete incident report

Major Spill (>1 Liter): Evacuate area, call Security x2222

6. PHYSICAL HAZARDS

Hazard Type Sources Controls PPE Required
Centrifuge High-speed rotation Balance loads, inspect tubes Face shield if aerosol risk
Autoclave Steam, pressure Training, cycle verification Heat-resistant gloves
Cryogenic Liquid nitrogen Ventilation, proper transfer Cryo gloves, face shield
UV Light Biosafety cabinets Shields, warning signs UV-blocking eyewear
Noise Centrifuges, alarms Distance, barriers Hearing protection if >85 dB
Sharps Needles, broken glass Safety devices, proper disposal Puncture-resistant gloves

Centrifuge Safety Protocol

  • Always balance tubes by weight, not volume
  • Inspect tubes for cracks before use
  • Never open during operation or before complete stop
  • Wait 10 minutes after aerosol-generating incident
  • Clean and disinfect rotors weekly

Work Area Gloves Lab Coat Eye Protection Additional
Specimen Processing Required Required Required Face shield for uncapping
Chemistry Required Required When splash risk Chemical-resistant as needed
Hematology Required Required When splash risk N/A
Microbiology Double gloves Required Required N95 for TB work
Blood Bank Required Required Required Fluid-resistant gown if needed
Molecular Required Dedicated coat When splash risk Separate pre/post-PCR PPE

PPE Donning and Doffing Sequence

Donning (Putting On):

  1. Perform hand hygiene
  2. Put on lab coat
  3. Put on face protection if required
  4. Put on gloves (over coat cuffs)

Doffing (Removing):

  1. Remove gloves (turn inside out)
  2. Remove face protection if worn
  3. Remove lab coat (turn inside out)
  4. Perform hand hygiene immediately

8. EXPOSURE CONTROL PLAN

IMMEDIATE RESPONSE TO BLOODBORNE PATHOGEN EXPOSURE

  1. Needlestick/Cut:
    • Wash with soap and water for 5 minutes
    • Do not squeeze wound
  2. Splash to Eyes/Nose/Mouth:
    • Flush with water/saline for 15 minutes
    • Use eyewash station
  3. Report Immediately:
    • Notify supervisor
    • Go to Employee Health or Emergency Department
    • Complete exposure incident report
  4. Follow-up:
    • Source patient testing if consent obtained
    • Post-exposure prophylaxis within 2 hours if indicated
    • Follow-up testing per protocol

TIME IS CRITICAL - PEP MOST EFFECTIVE WITHIN 2 HOURS

Exposure Type Risk Level Action Required Follow-up
Percutaneous (deep) High Immediate PEP evaluation 6-month monitoring
Percutaneous (superficial) Moderate Risk assessment Based on source status
Mucous membrane Moderate Immediate flush, evaluate Based on volume/source
Non-intact skin Low Wash, assess Usually none required
Intact skin None Wash area No follow-up needed

9. SPILL MANAGEMENT PROCEDURES

Biological Spill Response

  1. Alert: Notify others, post warning signs
  2. PPE: Don appropriate protective equipment
  3. Cover: Place paper towels over spill
  4. Disinfect: Pour 10% bleach, let stand 20 minutes
  5. Clean: Work from outside to center
  6. Dispose: Place all materials in biohazard bag
  7. Decontaminate: Clean area again with disinfectant
  8. Document: Complete incident report

Spill Kit Locations and Contents

12. FIRE SAFETY

R.A.C.E. Protocol for Fire Response

  • RESCUE - Remove anyone in immediate danger
  • ALARM - Activate fire alarm, call 911
  • CONTAIN - Close doors to contain fire/smoke
  • EVACUATE/EXTINGUISH - Evacuate or use extinguisher if trained

P.A.S.S. Method for Fire Extinguisher Use

  • PULL - Pull the pin
  • AIM - Aim at base of fire
  • SQUEEZE - Squeeze the handle
  • SWEEP - Sweep side to side

13. ELECTRICAL SAFETY

Electrical Hazard Prevention

  • Report damaged cords or plugs immediately
  • Never use equipment with exposed wires
  • Keep liquids away from electrical equipment
  • Don't overload circuits or daisy-chain power strips
  • Ensure hands are dry before handling electrical equipment
  • Unplug equipment before servicing
  • Use GFCI outlets near water sources

14. ERGONOMICS AND INJURY PREVENTION

Workstation Setup Guidelines

  • Monitor at eye level, arm's length away
  • Keyboard and mouse at elbow height
  • Feet flat on floor or footrest
  • Chair supporting lower back
  • Frequently used items within easy reach
  • Alternate sitting and standing when possible

Repetitive Strain Injury Prevention

Task Risk Factor Prevention Method
Pipetting Thumb strain Electronic pipettes, rotation
Microscopy Neck/eye strain Adjust eyepiece, take breaks
Computer work Carpal tunnel Ergonomic keyboard, stretches
Specimen processing Shoulder strain Adjust work height, rotate tasks
Lifting boxes Back injury Proper lifting technique, get help

15. INCIDENT REPORTING

Report ALL Incidents Within 24 Hours

Incidents requiring immediate reporting:

  • Any injury, no matter how minor
  • Exposure to blood or body fluids
  • Chemical exposures or spills
  • Near-miss events
  • Equipment malfunctions causing safety risk
  • Security breaches
